3.Methodology

Borehole samples and hydroacoustic data were applied to achieve the aim of this study. These data, collected from the back-barrier region of the Norderney Island, span over two years, from 2016 – 2018. The Holocene-Pleistocene boundary was initially detected in the cores collected over the study area and eventually matched with their corresponding hydroacoustic reflectors. This boundary interface, otherwise referred to as the Holocene base, was digitized along the sub-bottom hydroacoustic lines using the Innomar ISE post-processing software. The Geographic coordinates alongside varying depths ("XYZ" data) of each acoustic reflector aided the geostatistical modeling of a two-dimension elevation surface of the Holocene base of the Norderney back-barrier area.

  1. Data collection

    • Sub-bottom profiling

      • Hydroacoustic data were collected from the subtidal section of the back-barrier region of Norderney using the parametric sounder SES-2000 Standard Plus mounted on the Senckenberg research vessel of the Senckenberg Institute. The SES-2000 is a non-linear, high-resolution device developed by INNOMAR'S Technology. The first set of hydroacoustic lines (April 2016) acquired in the southern section of Norderney runs parallel to the Norderneyer Riffgatt channel. This channel is a tidal inlet connecting the Wadden Sea to the North Sea, and it cuts deep into the Holocene and Pleistocene deposits. The WASA group named three long acoustic lines collected during this survey period into South, North, and Central lines. As the research vessel traveled North-South along the Norderneyer Riffgatt channel, several crosslines (transects) were extracted over these lines. Additional transects parallel to the Busetief channel and perpendicular to the three long lines of 2016 were gathered in March and April of 2018 to provide complete acoustic information of the Island's subtidal region.

      Fig.5.Map of the back-barrier region of Norderney, indicating the different acoustic datasets extracted over two year survey period, 2016-2018.

    • Cores

      • Cores collected across the entire Norderney area during the sub-bottom profiling summed up to 156 samples. However, since this study is restricted to the Island's back-barrier region, the offshore cores are subtracted, bringing it down to 108 cores. These cores were collected in three survey periods, that is, from 2016 – 2018. Subtidal located cores were collected using a VKG-6 electronic vibrocorer installed on a crane aboard the RV Senckenberg and RV Buchanna, while a manual coring system was used in the intertidal areas. SeaPath System with applied RTK-corrections of the Konsberg Maritime was deployed for exact positioning.

      Fig.6. Map of the back-barrier region of Norderney, showing the borehole samples collected over three survey periods (2016-2018). The green-colored points represent cores with the Holocene base present, and the red points are cores without Holocene base.

    • Streif's Holocene base model

      • The Holocene base of the back-barrier region of Norderney proposed by Streif will be applied to this study. Streif's Holocene base was determined from borehole samples collected along Germany's North Sea coast by the Lower Saxon State Office for Soil Research (NLfB). This base will be recalculated into a new digital terrain model (DTM), compared, and integrated with the reconstructed Holocene base.

      Fig.7. Extract from the geographic base data of the NIBIS® Kartenserver (2014): Relief of the Holocene base of the Norderney's back-barrier area. - Landesamt für Bergbau, Energie und Geologie (LBEG), Hannover

    • LiDAR and Bathymetric data of the recent surface area

      • LiDAR stands for Light Detection and Ranging. It is a laser-based remote sensing system that directs a tiny laser at a subject and determines the time it takes for the laser to return to its source. This technology is used to create a digital elevation model (DEM) or a digital terrain model (DTM) for 3D mapping in geographical information systems (GIS). For this study, the LiDAR data for the intertidal and MBES-(Multibeam echosounder) based bathymetric data for the subtidal back-barrier section of the study area courtesy of the Lower Saxony State Office for Water Management, Coastal and Nature Conservation Coastal Research Center (NLWKN) will be utilized.

  2. Data processing and evaluation

    3. QGIS software aided the visualization of the location of cores and acoustic data on the Norderney base map. This software was essential in selecting cores with a Holocene base and their corresponding acoustic lines. The acoustic survey lines were selected based on a ten-meter (10m) offset correlation with cores having an established Holocene base and an excellent reflective quality. The acoustic lines were processed using the INNORMAR SES-2000 Post-Processing program ISE. Tide gauges and Sea depths were adjusted for individual acoustic lines selected based on their proximity to cores with the existing Holocene base. These cores were imported as ASCII files into the ISE software and overlaid on their corresponding acoustic lines. The boundary contact reflector between the Holocene and Pleistocene deposits resulting from merging both datasets was digitized for each selected transect. The values for longitudes, latitudes, and depths (NHN) were imported into the Surfer 21 software for further analysis in CSV format.     • Spatial analysis

      • Spatial analysis manipulates spatial data to extract new information and meaning from the original data. One way to generate new information is usually through spatial interpolations. Interpolation is a statistical process of utilizing data collected as point features such as GPS readings and borehole samples of a known region to estimate and extract information for nearby unknown regions. Generally, there are different interpolation methods, and no single method can solve all spatial problems. Furthermore, different methods produce different spatial results. Some are more exact and useful, while some could take longer to compute; hence, they all have merits and demerits. Applying an unsuitable method or inappropriate parameters can result in a distorted spatial distribution model, leading to potentially wrong decisions (Mitas & Mitasova, 1999).

      Nevertheless, it is advisable to try out several suitable methods for the available data type and select the best. Some of the most common interpolations are Kriging, Triangulation, Nearest Neighbor, Inverse distance weighting, and Natural Neighbor.
